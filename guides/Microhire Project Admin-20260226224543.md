# Scope

1\. The Client hereby agrees to engage the Consultant to provide the Client with the following consulting services (the "Services"):

SCOPE 1 - Proof of concept Business Intelligence (BI) Dashboard

The Client uses two primary systems to run the operations of the business. As they scale, there is a need to have intelligent insights drawn from the data in the systems. The Client has therefore recently implemented a BI tool, PowerBi, into their ecosystem. The data between their two core systems and PowerBi has been integrated. The Client is interested in testing the waters with the Consultant by requesting a singular Proof of Concept dashboard, with the following inclusions:
*   The Client’s 7 chosen metrics to be displayed on a Dashboard.
*   Definition process to ensure the parameters of each metric meet the Client’s needs at the detailed level.
*   Front-end to Back-end data mapping of the required fields for the chosen metrics.
*   Design and build the dashboard with the 7 metrics.

Note: A complete company-wide, historical, data cleansing exercise is NOT in scope of this Service. Only the data required to populate the chosen metrics will be reviewed for accuracy.

SCOPE 2 - AI Transactional sales agent

The Client expends significant human resource time on processing transactional, lower-priced sales tickets. A significant portion of these transactional sales can be automated in a sophisticated manner using AI and advanced technology built custom to the requirements of the Client. By allowing these transactions to be taken care of digitally, to an equivalent or greater standard than the sales resources, the Client can utilise the capacity created in their sales resources differently. Any time saved by sales resources now achieves a doubling positive effect on the Client’s financial performance. This is because the AI is making sales, and the sales resources are making more sales, all within the same period.

The Consultant will provide the following scope of services:
*   A build of custom technology, inclusive of AI, that is equipped to run an end-to-end automated sales process on specific transaction types. This includes:
    *   Custom software architecture to house the sales process.
    *   The integration of that architecture within the Client’s website, funnel pages and other spaces that require its access link.
    *   A dynamic AI agent or technology that is capable of asking specific sales questions and responding with additional questions based on the responses it receives.
    *   Creation of the sales process algorithm and human-like thought patterns to mimic the best salesperson’s behaviour.
    *   UI/UX design of the infrastructure to remain on brand of the Client.
    *   Collection and evaluation of the data and intel to be fed into the system to ensure quality and reliability of the processing engine.
    *   Training of the AI and technology to achieve the outcomes of this scope.
    *   Creation of the quote document format.
    *   Design and build of the digital sales workflow.
    *   Building upselling features into the system.
    *   Creation of the ‘brief’ document format for the operational team for execution.
    *   Building in escalation triggers so the system knows when to pass on the quote to the sales resource.
    *   Integration of the systems so that the quote data is digitally transferred to the booking system, as a booking, avoiding any manual data entry.
    *   Data from this system to be fed to the business dashboard to measure its efficacy, e.g. number of leads attempting a digital quote vs the number of quotes closed.
    *   Group training for the Client to become proficient with this new system.

2\. The Services will also include any other consulting tasks that the Parties may agree on. The Consultant hereby agrees to provide such Services to the Client.

# Microhire Sales Process

## Current Human Sales Process

Microhire starts the sales process by identifying the venue the Client wants to have their event in. This is not just the hotel or venue, but also the specific room that the event is likely to be held in. This is because each room is very different from each other. The size and shape of the room dictates the type of equipment required. Some rooms already have equipment pre-fitted into it. This means the hire of equipment for those rooms are generally limited to the fixed equipment and any mobile equipment the Client wants to hire.

Microhire has movable equipment stock as well. These are stored on Partner venues, and in warehouses controlled by Microhire. Microhire has all inventory items, including consumables (stock such as whiteboard markers that are not expected to be returned) managed in Rental Point. The location and the stock and the quantities of stock is all held in this live RP Database.

When a Client requests a quote, the next thing they need to look at are the dates. This is for availability of people and stock. There are often multiple quotes in the system, so Clients are granted their booking on a first in basis. The way the Rental Point system does this is as soon as the quote moves to the Heavy Pencil status, RP treats it as an unofficial booking and 'assigns' stock and people to that Client's booking. The other quotes that are in the system will now show that the availability no longer exists.

If this Heavy Pencil quote doesn't proceed, the availability becomes open again.

The sales rep then ascertains what type of Event the Client is having. They need to understand the requirements the Client "asks" for, but also what the client really "needs" to have a successful event. The Sales Rep provides advice, upsells and tries to close the sale.

About 90% of the quotes, where the Client has chosen to proceed with the Venue that incorporated Microhire, are quotes that are won.

When the Sales Rep enters the quote information into RP, they are required to show an itemised view of the equipment being "hired", however as this can be quite a lot of stock some times, it takes a while to do it one at a time. This is the reason packages were set up, they are essentially a group of assets in stock, that are hired regularly together. By selecting a package when quoting, the Sale Rep is able to do the quote much faster. Also in some cases, a package price is cheaper than the sum of its individual parts to hire. So it is more advantageous for a Client if there requirements fit into a 'package'. Even if a package is chosen, additional individual items can also be added.

The quote with the itemised assets, within the various Asset Categories chosen by Microhire find their way on a quote via RP. The "manage booking" screen is where the quote data gets entered, and then when complete, RP produces a basic looking quote. There are style limitations in RP, hence the quotes look the way they do.

The Sales Rep potentially can go back and forth until the Client agrees to proceed. There is then some paperwork to sign.

## ✅ 1. **Store and Index Your Private Data Effectively**
Use **Azure Cognitive Search** (or another vector store) to ingest and index your private data.
*   **Preprocess**: Clean, chunk, and format your documents (use semantic chunks, not just page-by-page)
*   **Embed**: Use Azure OpenAI embedding models to convert chunks into vectors
*   **Store**: Store vectors + metadata in a searchable index
*   **Update**: Regularly sync your data source for freshness
👉 This makes your data retrievable by relevance when a question is asked.
## ✅ 2. **Retrieve Relevant Data Dynamically at Runtime**
When the user asks something:
*   Use **semantic search** (or hybrid retrieval) to pull the top N most relevant chunks
*   Avoid pulling unrelated or too many chunks — too much info can **dilute** the prompt
## ✅ 3. **Inject Retrieved Data Smartly Into the LLM Prompt**
This is **memory/context injection**, and it’s crucial.
You must **prepend the retrieved private content** into the prompt like:

```sql
plaintext
CopyEdit
System Prompt:
You are a helpful assistant. Use the provided context below to answer.

Context:
[Start of retrieved chunks...]
- Document 1: ...
- Document 2: ...
[End of retrieved chunks]

User Question:
"Can I file a claim after 60 days?"
```

### 🔥 Key Tips:
*   **Tell the model to rely only on the provided context.**
*   Use instructions like:
    *   “Answer based only on the context.”
    *   “If the answer is not in the context, say you don’t know.”
*   Limit fallback to pretraining unless necessary.
## ✅ 4. **Use a Strong Prompt Template (with grounding bias)**

```plain
plaintext
CopyEdit
System Prompt Example:
You are a company assistant helping users based on internal policy documents.
Use ONLY the context below to answer questions. If the answer is not found in the context, respond: "I'm sorry, that information is not available."

Context:
[Top 3 retrieved chunks]

User: [actual user question]
```

* * *
## ✅ 5. **Avoid Hallucination by Controlling Temperature**
When you call the LLM (Azure OpenAI):
*   Set **temperature = 0** to make responses deterministic and grounded
*   This reduces hallucination and speculation
* * *
## ✅ 6. **Don’t Overfill the Context Window**
Even GPT-4 Turbo has a **context limit (e.g., 128K tokens)**. Injecting too much:
*   Makes responses vague or distracted
*   Pushes important info out of the window
✅ Use:
*   Top 3–5 chunks (e.g., 500–1000 words max)
*   Summarize if needed
*   Deduplicate overlapping chunks
* * *
## ✅ 7. **Monitor and Evaluate**
Log:
*   Which chunks were retrieved
*   What the model responded with
*   Whether it correctly referenced your data
You can also:
*   Use **citations** in responses
*   Run grounding/faithfulness evaluation (Microsoft’s Copilot Studio helps with this)
* * *
## ✅ 8. **Optional: Disable Pretrained Knowledge (Soft Control)**
While GPT-4 can't be fully disabled from using its training, you can:
*   Use strict system prompts to constrain it
*   Block unrelated content from showing up in results
*   Use prompt chaining with post-validation (e.g., check if retrieved data was cited)

# Connect AI Agent to SQL Server

Yes, using **Azure AI Studio (formerly Azure AI Foundry)** and **Agents**, you **can connect to SQL Server** and retrieve data — **but not directly from within the agent's instruction box alone**.
### Here's how it works:
#### ✅ **What you can do:**
1. **Use Azure Functions or APIs** to act as a bridge between your agent and SQL Server.
2. **Connect your agent to that function/API** using a **tool** in the Azure AI Agent config.
3. **Pass parameters from the conversation** (like client ID, product, or quote request) to the tool, which then performs the SQL operation.
* * *
### 🔧 How to set it up:
#### 1\. **Create an Azure Function or REST API**
*   This API will contain logic to:
    *   Connect to your SQL Server
    *   Execute a query (e.g., `SELECT * FROM Products WHERE Category = 'AV Equipment'`)
    *   Return results in JSON
#### 2\. **Deploy it to Azure (Function App, App Service, etc.)**
#### 3\. **Add it to the Agent as a Tool**
In Azure AI Studio:
*   Go to your **Agent settings**
*   Under Action, click the + Add at the right
*   Select OpenAPI 3.0 specified tool
*   Provide:
    *   The **API endpoint**
    *   **OpenAPI schema** or REST details (URL, method, parameters)
#### 4\. **Use natural language to call that tool**
From within the Agent prompt, the agent can call your SQL API indirectly. For example:

```plain
Tool: GetProductDetails
Input: "Get the AV product pricing for microphone and speakers"
→ Backend: Calls SQL Server via API and returns results
```

* * *
### 📌 Important:
*   **Direct SQL connections from within agents are not allowed** for security reasons.
*   You **must go through a tool/API layer**.
*   This approach gives you control over **permissions**, **rate limiting**, and **query safety**.
* * *
### Example Use Case:
> 🗣️ _“The client wants 4 wireless microphones and 2 speakers. Get the total cost.”_  
> Agent → Calls your tool → Tool queries SQL → Agent formats the quote response.

# Integrating DeepSeek API to React JS

**1\. Obtain a DeepSeek API Key:**
Sign up or log in to your DeepSeek account.
Navigate to the API key management section (usually found in account settings or developer dashboard).
Generate a new API key and copy it. Crucially, never expose this key directly in your client-side React code or public repositories. Use environment variables or a backend proxy for secure handling.

**2\. Install a HTTP Client (e.g., Axios):**
Axios is a popular promise-based HTTP client for the browser and Node.js. Install it in your React project:

    npm install axios

**3\. Create an API Service File:**
Create a dedicated file (e.g., src/services/deepseekService.js) to encapsulate your API calls. This promotes code organization and reusability.

    `// src/services/deepseekService.js`
    `import axios from 'axios';`

    `const DEEPSEEK_API_URL = 'YOUR_DEEPSEEK_API_ENDPOINT'; // Replace with the specific DeepSeek API endpoint`
    `const DEEPSEEK_API_KEY = process.env.REACT_APP_DEEPSEEK_API_KEY; // Use environment variables`

    `export const fetchDeepSeekResponse = async (query) => {`
        `try {`
            `const response = await` [`axios.post`](http://axios.post)`(DEEPSEEK_API_URL, {`
                `// Your DeepSeek API request body (e.g., for chat completion)`
                `messages: [{ role: 'user', content: query }],`
                `model: 'deepseek-chat', // Specify the DeepSeek model`
            `}, {`
                `headers: {`
                    ``'Authorization': `Bearer ${DEEPSEEK_API_KEY}`,``
                    `'Content-Type': 'application/json',`
                `},`
            `});`
            `return` [`response.data`](http://response.data)`;`
        `} catch (error) {`
            `console.error('Error fetching DeepSeek response:', error);`
            `throw error; // Re-throw to handle in component`
        `}`
    `};`

4\. Integrate into a React Component:
Import the service function into your React component and use it to fetch data. Manage loading states and errors within your component.

    `// src/components/DeepSeekChat.js`
    `import React, { useState } from 'react';`
    `import { fetchDeepSeekResponse } from '../services/deepseekService';`

    `const DeepSeekChat = () => {`
        `const [userMessage, setUserMessage] = useState('');`
        `const [botResponse, setBotResponse] = useState('');`
        `const [loading, setLoading] = useState(false);`

        `const handleSendMessage = async () => {`
            `if (!userMessage.trim()) return;`

            `setLoading(true);`
            `try {`
                `const data = await fetchDeepSeekResponse(userMessage);`
                `setBotResponse(data.choices[0].message.content); // Adjust based on DeepSeek API response structure`
            `} catch (error) {`
                `setBotResponse('Error: Could not get a response from DeepSeek.');`
            `} finally {`
                `setLoading(false);`
                `setUserMessage('');`
            `}`
        `};`

        `return (`
            `<div>`
                `<input`
                    `type="text"`
                    `value={userMessage}`
                    `onChange={(e) => setUserMessage(e.target.value)}`
                    `placeholder="Type your message..."`
                `/>`
                `<button onClick={handleSendMessage} disabled={loading}>`
                    `{loading ? 'Sending...' : 'Send'}`
                `</button>`
                `{botResponse && <div>Bot: {botResponse}</div>}`
            `</div>`
        `);`
    `};`

    `export default DeepSeekChat;`

5\. Use the Component in your App:
Import your DeepSeek component into your main App.js or any other relevant parent component.

    `// src/App.js`
    `import React from 'react';`
    `import DeepSeekChat from './components/DeepSeekChat';`

    `function App() {`
        `return (`
            `<div className="App">`
                `<h1>DeepSeek Integration Example</h1>`
                `<DeepSeekChat />`
            `</div>`
        `);`
    `}`

    `export default App;`

## MicroHire’s Timing Language
Below are details of the language Microhire use for event timings.

is an explanation of the terms we use when discussing event schedules:
*   **Venue Access Time**
    *   This is the time when our team is allowed into the venue to begin unloading and setting up equipment. It is important to confirm this with your venue so that we do not face delays at the start of the day.
*   **Set-Up By**
    *   This is the time by which all AV equipment must be fully installed, tested, and ready to use. In many cases, this will be just before guests arrive or before the rehearsal begins.
*   **Rehearsal or Run-Through**
    *   This is scheduled time for presenters, performers, or the event team to test microphones, lighting, presentations, and stage movements. It ensures everyone is comfortable with the equipment and allows us to make any last-minute adjustments.
*   **Doors Open or Guest Arrival**
    *   The time when attendees are allowed to enter the event space. All equipment should already be running smoothly by this stage.
*   **Event Start**
    *   The official beginning of your program, such as the first speaker, the MC’s welcome, or the start of entertainment. This is when all cues, sound, and visuals must be perfectly timed.
*   **Breaks (Morning Tea, Lunch, Afternoon Tea, etc.)**
    *   Break times are important for us to know because they affect how we manage background music, microphone checks, and operator shifts. For longer events, these breaks also provide opportunities for equipment adjustments if required.
*   **Event End**
    *   The official conclusion of the program, which is often followed by audience departure. Our team remains onsite until the end of the program to ensure everything runs as expected.
*   **Pack-Down or Load-Out**
    *   The time when we are allowed to start dismantling and removing equipment. This may be immediately after the event or the following day, depending on venue rules and your requirements.
By providing us with a detailed run sheet using this timing language, you help us align our technical planning with your event flow.

## Seasonality and AV Availability in Australia
The availability of AV equipment and crew depends heavily on the time of year. Some periods are extremely busy for events across Australia, while others are quieter. Understanding this seasonal demand will help you plan ahead and avoid disappointment.
### Peak Season (High Demand)
*   **September to early December**: This is the busiest time of the year. Corporate awards nights, gala dinners, Christmas parties, and end-of-year functions fill the calendar.
*   **Late January to March**: After the summer holiday break, business events return in full force. This includes conferences, exhibitions, and product launches.
During peak season, it is strongly recommended to book your AV requirements well in advance—ideally 8 to 12 weeks before your event. Equipment and crew are in high demand, so early planning secures your preferred setup.
### Normal Season (Steady Demand)
*   **April to June**: Many organisations hold conferences, workshops, and training programs during this period.
*   **July to August**: Mid-year corporate events, meetings, and industry expos are common.
In these months, availability is generally strong, but large-scale productions should still be booked at least 4 to 6 weeks in advance.
### Lean Season (Lower Demand)
*   **Mid-December to mid-January**: This is the quietest period in the event calendar. Many companies are closed for the holiday break, and corporate events are minimal.
During the lean season, equipment and crew availability is high. It is possible to secure AV services at shorter notice, but keep in mind that some venues may be closed or operating with limited staff.

# B. Time Allocation for Events
#### Test & Connect / Rehearsal
*   Minimum duration: 30 minutes.
*   Standard recommendation: 60 minutes to allow for contingencies.
*   Options to offer: 30, 45, or 60 minutes.
*   For multi-day events, schedule Test & Connect sessions for each day.

#### Setup and Packdown
*   **Setup Time:**
    *   Minimum setup charge: 3 hours (due to labor hour requirements).
    *   Light setups: 30, 45, or 60 minutes.
    *   Heavy setups: 3-8 hours, depending on logistics (e.g., loading/unloading, elevator access).
    *   Example: Meeting rooms at Westin typically require 30-60 minutes for setup.
*   **Packdown Time:**
    *   Estimated as half the setup time, rounded up to the next hour.
*   Always assume longer durations to account for unforeseen delays.
*   Match the number of technicians involved in setup and packdown to the job’s requirements.

# Event Themes
Choosing a theme is one of the best ways to give an event personality and direction. A good theme helps guests know what to expect, guides décor and styling, and sets the overall atmosphere. Below are common event types with the themes usually requested, explained in detail.
* * *
## 1\. Gala Event
**Popular Themes:**
*   **Black Tie / Formal Elegance** – A traditional gala style with men in tuxedos and women in gowns. Décor often includes chandeliers, candlelit tables, crisp white or black linens, and live orchestral or jazz music. This creates a luxurious and timeless atmosphere.
*   **Masquerade Ball** – Guests wear elegant masks, creating mystery and drama. Decor features rich jewel tones like deep purple, emerald, and gold, with velvet drapes and ornate centrepieces. Perfect for charity balls or fundraisers.

# **Safety & Compliance in Events (AV Focus)**
## Why Safety & Compliance Matters
Events bring together people, technology, and logistics — often in high-pressure environments. Audio-visual (AV) setups involve heavy equipment, power, cabling, staging, and lighting, which, if not handled correctly, can cause safety risks.
At MicroHire, safety and compliance are non-negotiable. Every event must meet **legal, venue, and occupational health & safety (OH&S)** requirements to protect attendees, staff, and organisers.
* * *
## Key Areas of AV Safety & Compliance
### 1\. **Electrical & Power Safety**
*   Use only tested and tagged equipment.
*   Ensure correct power load distribution (avoid overloading circuits).
*   Keep power boards, extension leads, and connections away from public walkways.
### 2\. **Rigging & Staging Safety**
*   Only qualified riggers should install overhead lighting, trusses, or hanging screens.
*   Stages, risers, and platforms must meet weight-bearing standards.
*   Always include handrails and ramps where required.
### 3\. **Cabling & Trip Hazards**
*   Secure all cables with proper covers, mats, or cable ramps.
*   Never run loose cables across walkways or emergency exits.
*   Use clearly visible tape or covers in darker environments.
### 4\. **Fire & Emergency Compliance**
*   Do not block fire exits, extinguishers, or emergency signage with AV setups.
*   All draping, theming, and staging materials must be flame-retardant.
*   Ensure venue evacuation procedures are known by crew and operators.
### 5\. **Noise & Lighting Safety**
*   Keep sound levels within venue and legal limits to prevent hearing damage.
*   Avoid excessive strobe lighting that may trigger medical issues (e.g., epilepsy).
*   Provide clear warnings if such effects are part of the show.
### 6\. **Work Health & Safety (WHS)**

### 7\. **Insurance & Documentation**
*   Public liability insurance must cover all suppliers.
*   Risk assessments and method statements (SWMS) should be completed before setup.
*   Venue induction and compliance documents must be signed off.

## **Do’s and Don’ts for AV Safety**

*   Do use proper cable management solutions to prevent hazards.

### Don’ts

*   Don’t overload power boards, piggyback plugs, or use damaged cables.

# AV Risks & Potential Issues ✅

# **AV Risks and Potential Issues at Events**
### **1\. Audio Problems**
*   **Feedback or Distortion:** Occurs when microphones pick up sound from nearby speakers. Can ruin speeches or performances.
*   **Dead Microphones or Low Battery:** Wireless mics can fail mid-event if batteries aren’t charged.
*   **Uneven Audio Levels:** Some areas too loud or too quiet due to improper speaker placement.
*   **Interference:** Wireless equipment can pick up signals from other devices, causing static or dropouts.
*   **Operator Error:** Technicians might misadjust mixing consoles, causing poor sound quality.
**Prevention Tips:** Always test microphones and speakers, keep backup batteries, do a full soundcheck, and have a trained operator monitor audio during the event.
* * *
### **2\. Visual & Video Problems**
*   **Projector or Screen Malfunction:** Can occur due to connectivity issues, lamp failure, or misalignment.
*   **LED Wall or Display Failure:** Pixels or panels may stop working if power or cabling issues occur.
*   **Camera or Recording Glitches:** PTZ cameras may freeze or lose connection during streaming.
*   **Incorrect Video Scaling:** Images may appear stretched, cropped, or blurry if input resolution isn’t matched.
*   **Operator Errors:** Wrong video source displayed or transitions mistimed.
**Prevention Tips:** Test all displays, calibrate resolutions, run full video rehearsals, and assign trained operators. Always have backup connections ready.
* * *
### **3\. Lighting Issues**
*   **Power Failure:** Lights may go out if circuits are overloaded.
*   **Incorrect Programming:** Cue timing or color changes may be off if lighting scenes are not rehearsed.
*   **Equipment Malfunction:** Moving heads or intelligent lighting can fail if DMX connections are loose or controllers misconfigured.
*   **Glare or Blinding:** Lights may shine into cameras or audience if positioned incorrectly.
**Prevention Tips:** Inspect circuits, rehearse lighting sequences, secure all fixtures, and have an operator actively monitor cues.
* * *
### **4\. Stage & Rigging Failures**
*   **Unstable or Collapsing Structures:** Poor assembly or overloading trusses can cause accidents.
*   **Motorised Stage Malfunctions:** Risers or lifts may fail if motors are miscalibrated.
*   **Backdrop or Truss Detachment:** Loose rigging can fall, causing damage or injury.
*   **Obstructed Sightlines:** Improper stage setup may block audience views.
**Prevention Tips:** Inspect all rigging points, assemble stages by trained crew, and test motorised systems before the event.
* * *
### **5\. Interactive & Engagement Failures**
*   **Device Connectivity Issues:** Voting systems, LED wristbands, or AR/VR devices may lose sync.
*   **Software Crashes:** Apps or kiosks may freeze under heavy use.
*   **Participant Confusion:** Guests may not understand how to use interactive tech.
*   **Hardware Malfunction:** Touchscreens or headsets may stop responding.
**Prevention Tips:** Test all devices, have backup hardware, provide staff assistance, and run a rehearsal for audience interaction.
* * *
### **6\. Special Effects & Atmospheric Risks**
*   **Safety Hazards:** Fog, haze, pyrotechnics, or CO2 jets can be dangerous if used incorrectly.
*   **Timing Issues:** Effects fired too early or late can ruin cues or visual impact.
*   **Malfunction:** Machines may fail to start or release effects incorrectly.
*   **Venue Restrictions:** Effects may violate fire or safety codes.
**Prevention Tips:** Follow manufacturer instructions, rehearse cues, assign trained operators, and verify compliance with venue rules.
* * *
### **7\. Power & Infrastructure Failures**
*   **Overloaded Circuits:** Multiple high-power devices can trip breakers.
*   **Generator Failure:** Backup power may not start during an outage.
*   **Cable Hazards:** Poorly laid cables may cause trips, disconnect devices, or short circuits.
*   **Voltage Fluctuations:** Can damage sensitive AV equipment.
**Prevention Tips:** Calculate loads, use surge protectors, secure cables, and test backup power systems.
* * *
### **8\. Operator or Human Error**
*   **Incorrect Cue Execution:** Audio, lighting, or video cues may be mistimed.
*   **Equipment Misuse:** Staff may improperly set up devices, causing damage or malfunction.
*   **Communication Gaps:** Misalignment between AV, stage management, and organisers.
**Prevention Tips:** Train all operators, run full rehearsals, provide cue sheets, and maintain active communication during the event.
* * *
### **9\. Environmental or Venue-Related Issues**
*   **Ambient Noise:** External sounds may interfere with audio clarity.
*   **Lighting Interference:** Sunlight or reflections may reduce screen visibility.
*   **Space Constraints:** Limited room can make equipment setup difficult.
*   **Temperature & Humidity:** Can affect delicate electronic equipment.
**Prevention Tips:** Conduct a venue inspection, plan equipment placement carefully, and ensure climate control or protective covers for devices.
